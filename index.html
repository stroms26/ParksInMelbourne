It is January in Melbourne, and the asphalt is breathing heat. Tram Stop 7C, sitting beside the Tennis Centre, is the warmest place in our dataset; Royal Park, a kilometre away under a canopy of river red gums, is the coolest. Between them, eleven sensors quietly log the temperature, humidity, wind, and air quality of the city, every few minutes, every day of the year.

This essay tests an old urban-planning intuition with a young dataset: that parks measurably cool the city around them. The intuition is right—but the story is more interesting than a single number. The cooling effect varies: by hour, by season, by how hot the day already is. By the end you should know not just whether Melbourne’s parks cool the CBD, but when they matter most, and where the city is paying the heaviest price for going without them.

The Network

Eleven sensors, three environments

The City of Melbourne’s open microclimate network places identical sensors in three sharply different settings: inside parks, at street level among the trams and buildings, and high up on city rooftops. We treat the rooftops as a kind of control—they tell us what the air looks like above the street canyon. The fight for our story is between the parks and the streets at ground level.

Finding I · The Temperature Gap

Streets run hotter than parks — especially at the worst time of day

The clearest signal in the data is also the simplest. Take the months of December, January and February, restrict yourself to the hours between 1pm and 5pm, and compute the average temperature at every sensor in every class. Streets land at  °C; parks at  °C. A gap of  °C, every summer afternoon, year after year.

That sounds small. It isn’t. Average it across thousands of afternoons and millions of footsteps and it becomes the difference between a city that is uncomfortable and one that is dangerous. The shape of the day below explains why: the gap opens at sunrise, runs through the working day, and closes only when the sun does.

Finding II · The Humidity Inversion

Parks breathe. Streets don’t.

Temperature is only half of what makes a place feel cool. The other half is humidity—and on this measure the gap between parks and streets is much wider. Across all hours of the year, parks hold + percentage points more relative humidity than streets nearby. The mechanism is well understood: trees and grass evaporate water through their leaves all day, lifting humidity and lowering air temperature in the same breath.

If air temperature is the city’s pulse, humidity is its respiration. Look at the daily profile: parks track noticeably moister than streets at every hour, and the gap is largest in the early afternoon—exactly when people are out in it.

Figure 3. Mean relative humidity by hour of day, averaged across all months. Parks remain consistently more humid than streets at every hour; rooftops sit in between because they sample the bulk atmosphere above the canyon.

Figure 4. Park−street humidity advantage, by hour. Positive values mean parks are more humid than streets at that hour; the bar climbs through the morning and peaks in mid-afternoon.

Finding III · The Heatwave Amplifier

The hotter it gets, the more the parks matter

If the cooling gap were a fixed quantity—a third of a degree, every day, regardless of conditions—parks would be a small but predictable bonus. They aren’t fixed. They get bigger when you need them most.

On the 600 ordinary days in our window, the average daytime gap between street and park sensors is 0.15 °C. On the 21 days where the citywide maximum cleared 35 °C—the threshold the Bureau of Meteorology uses for an "extreme heat" day—the gap jumps to 0.42 °C. Parks stop being a perk and start being a refuge.

Figure 5. Each dot is one day. Horizontal axis: citywide daily maximum temperature. Vertical axis: that day’s afternoon street−park temperature gap. The trend line slopes up — hotter days, bigger cooling effect. Toggle the checkbox to filter to the 21 heatwave days.

Parks deliver their largest dividend on exactly the days when their absence is most lethal.Finding III · H3
Finding IV · Day and Night

The cooling gap is a 24-hour story — and the night shift surprises

We expected the cooling effect to be a daytime phenomenon: parks visibly shading themselves, streets baking in the sun. The data tells a more interesting story. Parks are cooler than streets at every hour of the day, but the gap is largest in the small hours of the night, when streets keep radiating the heat they absorbed during the working day and parks have already let theirs go.

This is the urban heat island effect made local: hard surfaces and buildings store heat through the day and release it slowly after sunset. Vegetation does not. The chart below traces the cooling signal hour by hour—at midnight the streets are still 0.33 °C warmer than the parks; at 4 pm the gap narrows to 0.09 °C.

Figure 6. Hourly cooling signal: street−park temperature, averaged over all matched hours. Positive everywhere — parks never lose — but the magnitude swings on a familiar 24-hour cycle.

Geography of Heat

Where the city runs hot

Step out of the time series and onto the map. The interactive view below plots every sensor on the streets of Melbourne, weighted by its summer-afternoon mean temperature. Cool blues cluster around the parks of the western edge—Royal Park, Batman Park, Birrarung Marr—and the dense red sits where the city grid is hardest: the tram stops near the Tennis Centre, the long radiating asphalt of Tram Stop 7C. The spread between coolest and hottest sensor is roughly 1.4 °C: a meaningful difference for any pedestrian crossing the CBD on a summer afternoon.

What It Means

A small number with public-health teeth

A third of a degree is too small to feel. It is not too small to matter. Heat is the deadliest weather hazard in Australia—more than bushfires, more than floods—and the burden falls disproportionately on the elderly, the unhoused, and people without air conditioning at home. Cooling is not an aesthetic question for these groups; it is a survival question. Two findings in this essay translate directly into planning advice:

The benefit grows where the risk is highest. Parks pull furthest ahead of streets on heatwave days—exactly the days that fill emergency departments. Investment in canopy cover and parkland is, statistically, an investment in heatwave resilience.

The benefit lasts after dark. The largest park–street gaps in our data are at night, when overnight minimum temperatures determine whether a body has time to recover from the day. Parks measurably weaken the urban heat island in the very hours that most threaten vulnerable residents.

What the data cannot yet say is which trees deliver the cooling, in what density, at what age. Our network only sees outcomes—air temperature in eleven places. A canopy-resolved analysis (every park’s species mix, crown diameter, and percentage cover within walking distance of each sensor) is the natural next step; the City of Melbourne’s tree register makes it possible. We leave that for the next issue.

Behind the story

Data: City of Melbourne Microclimate Sensor Readings open dataset, to , downloaded as a single CSV. We retain the 11 sensor sites with continuous coverage and complete air-temperature and humidity fields, totalling observations.

Site classification: sensors were tagged park / street / rooftop based on their installation context. The four parks are Birrarung Marr, Enterprize Park, Royal Park and Batman Park; the four street sensors are clustered around tram stops and the Treasury; the three rooftops sit on 101 Collins, CH1 and the MCEC SkyFarm.

Aggregation: raw readings were resampled to hourly means per site, then averaged again across sites within each class. The "summer afternoon" window is December–February, 13:00–17:00 local time; "heatwave day" is any day whose citywide maximum exceeded 35 °C.
